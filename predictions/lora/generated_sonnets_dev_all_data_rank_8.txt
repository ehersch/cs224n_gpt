Thine eyes I love, and they, as pitying me,
Knowing thy heart torment me with disdain,
Have put on black, and loving mourners be,
Who I am, they have to say: a shadow of whom I do lie,
Whose soul shall never, ever, ever fade,
And leave them in this miserable pain.
For what the hell have I stolen from thee now,
Thou art but a shadow, in this world that dwells,
And none can make me truly weep,
Nor pity me, nor this, nor that,
For when in my own, I know thy purer joys do grow.
   For in these lives, though love might fade and fade,
   Thy beauty shall never cease.

Beshrew that heart that makes my heart to groan
For that deep wound it gives my friend and me.
Is 't not enough to torture me alone,
And then do I pray thy name for pardon
And banish the thorny vines of all fear.
For if it is the soul of me, or thy dear friend,
Thy soul may well be one with you as well,
That binds together the very heavens.
So love itself cannot beat the lash of fate,
Or burn with flame from the flames of my fire,
Nor by the flames itself but by me can
Be the one true cause I keep so dear.
   So though my love may in vain destroy thy heart,

Lo, as a careful huswife runs to catch
One of her feathered creatures broke away,
Sets down her babe, and makes all swift dispatch
Of all she can think upon her most beloved sight.
It may fetch a purse for some poor purse,
Or steal a leaf of the flowers,
Or weave her own silk so it appears so,
She, for who, hath seen the beauty of thy life,
Lied there unseen, and from thy grace hid,
And cannot ever have left thee home so near.
  So she does not give the prize for her favours,
  The prize that her lover may bring forth;
  Her true love, the best that man can ask,
  A love that never shall be undone.
